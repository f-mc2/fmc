Change of coordinates

  1. Recalling how the [[2.1 From 1 to many dimensions#^1f484e|cylindrical]] and [[2.1 From 1 to many dimensions#^b5d3f4|spherical]] coordinates are expressed in terms of the Cartesian coordinates, find the function determining the passage from cylindrical to spherical coordinates and its inverse.
    >[!note]- Solution: > Compose \(\psi_{cyl}\) of [[2.1 From 1 to many dimensions#^1f484e|cylindrical coordinates]] with \(\psi^{-1}_{sph}\) of [[2.1 From 1 to many dimensions#^b5d3f4|spherical coordinates]] to get how cylindrical coordinates are expressed in terms of spherical coordinates. Exchange the role of \(\psi\) and \(\psi^{-1}\) to obtain how spherical coordinates are expressed using cylindrical coordinates.

  2. Determine the expression in cylindrical and spherical coordinates of the function \((D,\mathbb{R}^{3},f,\mathbb{R})\) expressed in Cartesian coordinates by: \[ \begin{split} 1) &\qquad f(x,y,z)= x^{2} + y^{2} +z^{2} \\ & \\ 2) &\qquad f(x,y,z)=\left(x,\;y-z,\; 3 \right) \\ & \\ 3) & \qquad f(x,y,z)= \mathrm{e}^{x^{2} + y^{2} +z} . \end{split} \] >[!note]- Solution: >Compose \(f\) with \(\psi^{-1}\) of [[2.1 From 1 to many dimensions#^1f484e|cylindrical coordinates]] or [[2.1 From 1 to many dimensions#^b5d3f4|spherical coordinates]].

  3. If the expression of \(f\colon\mathbb{R}^{2}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) in polar coordinates is \[ f(r,\theta) = r + \theta , \] determine the value of \(f\) a the point \(\mathbf{p}\) with Cartesian coordinates \((p_{x}=0, p_{y}=1)\), \((p_{x}=1,p_{y}=1)\), and \((p_{x}=2,p_{y}=3)\). >[!note]- Solution >In Cartesian coordinates, the point \((0,1)\) corresponds to the point \((1,\pi/2)\) in polar coordinates, while the point \((2,3)\) corresponds to the point \((\sqrt{13},\arctan(3/2))\).

  4. Let \((\mathbb{R},\mathbb{R},f, \mathbb{R})\) be expressed as \[ f(x)=\frac{\mathrm{e}^{2x} - 2 + \mathrm{e}^{-2x}}{4} \] in Cartesian coordinates. Determine a change of coordinate \((A,\mathbb{R},\psi,\mathbb{R})\) leading to \[ f\circ\psi(v) = v^{2} \] and discuss its domain of applicability. >[!note]- Solution >Note that \(\mathrm{e}^{2x} - 2 + \mathrm{e}^{-2x}=(\mathrm{e}^{x}-\mathrm{e}^{-x})^{2}\). The change of coordinates can be understood as \(x(v)=\psi(v)\), and we must have >\[ \frac{(\mathrm{e}^{x}-\mathrm{e}^{-x})^{2}}{4}=f\circ\psi(v)=v^{2},\] >which means we can take \(\psi(v)=\frac{\mathrm{e}^{x}-\mathrm{e}^{-x}}{2}\). This change of coordinates is valid for all \(v\) and \(x\) because both the domain and range of \(\psi\) are \(\mathbb{R}\). Note that \(\tilde{\psi}(v)=-\psi(v)\) is another, perfectly valid choice.

  5. Let \(f\colon\mathbb{R}^{2}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be expressed as \[ f(x,y)= ax + by \] in Cartesian coordinates with \(a,b\neq 0\). Determine a change of coordinate \(\psi\colon A\subseteq \mathbb{R}^{2}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{2}\) leading to \[ f\circ\psi(v,w)= v^2 - w^{3},\] and discuss its domain of applicability. Is \(\psi\) unique? >[!note]- Solution >There are infinitely many change of coordinates satisfying the constraint imposed. Indeed, we can take \(\psi(v,w)=(x(v,w),y(v,w))\) with \(x(v,w)=\alpha v^{2} +\beta w^{3}\), \(y(v,w)=\gamma v^{2} +\delta w^{3}\) and \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta\) satisfying \(a(\alpha + \gamma )=1\) and \(b(\beta + \delta)=-1\). In order for \(\psi\) to be a valid change of coordinates, we must impose that \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta\) are such that \(\psi\) can be inverted somewhere, and this determines the domain of applicability. For instance, taking \(\alpha=1/a\), \(\delta=-1/b\), and \(\beta=\gamma=0\), we have that \(\psi^{-1}\) only exists when \(x\geq 0\). On the other hand, if we take \(\alpha=\beta=0\), \(\gamma=1/a\), and \(\delta=-1/b\), then \(\psi^{-1}\) does not exist because \((v,w)=\psi^{-1}\circ\psi(v,w)=\psi^{-1}(0,v^{2}+w^{3})\) is impossible.

Limits of multivariable functions

  1. Prove the following limits: \[ \begin{split} 1) &\qquad \lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (0,0)}\,\frac{x^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}}=0 \\ & \\ 2) &\qquad \lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (0,0)}\,\frac{2x^{2}\,y}{x^{2} + y^{2}}=0 \\ & \\ 3) & \qquad \lim_{x\rightarrow 1} \;(x^{2},\;\mathrm{e}^{x^{3} -1}) = (1,\;1) \\ & \\ 4) & \qquad \lim_{(x,y,z)\rightarrow (0,0,0)}\,\frac{xyz}{x^{2} + y^{2} + z^{2}} =0 \\ & \\ 5) & \qquad \lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (0,0)}\,\frac{xy}{x^{2} +y^{2} +2}=0. \end{split} \] >[!note]- Solution >1) We have to prove that, for every \(\epsilon>0\), there is \(\delta_{\epsilon}>0\) such that \(\Vert\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{0}\Vert=\sqrt{(x-0)^{2}+(y-0)^{2}}=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}<\delta\) implies > \[ \vert\frac{x^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\vert<\epsilon .\] > At this purpose, we note that > \[ x^{2}\leq x^{2} + y^{2}\Longrightarrow \vert\frac{x^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\vert\leq \vert\frac{x^{2}+y^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} \vert=\vert \sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}} \vert \] > so that \(\delta_{\epsilon}<\epsilon\) does the job. >2) We have to prove that, for every \(\epsilon>0\), there is \(\delta_{\epsilon}>0\) such that \(\Vert\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{0}\Vert=\sqrt{(x-0)^{2}+(y-0)^{2}}=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}<\delta\) implies > \[ \vert\frac{2x^{2}y}{ x^{2}+y^{2} }\vert<\epsilon .\] > At this purpose, we note that \(x^{2}\leq x^{2} + y^{2}\) and \(|y|=\sqrt{y^{2}}\leq \sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}\), so that > \[ \vert\frac{2x^{2}y}{ x^{2}+y^{2} }\vert\leq \frac{2(x^{2}+y^{2})\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}}{ x^{2}+y^{2} }=2 \sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}, \] > and thus \(\delta_{\epsilon}<\epsilon\) does the job. >3) In this case, we have a vector function whose components are continuous functions for all \(x\in\mathbb{R}\). Therefore, the limit can be found by simply putting \(x=1\). >4) We have to prove that, for every \(\epsilon>0\), there is \(\delta_{\epsilon}>0\) such that \(\Vert\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{0}\Vert=\sqrt{(x-0)^{2}+(y-0)^{2} + (z-0)^{2}}=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}}<\delta\) implies > \[ \vert \frac{xyz}{x^{2} + y^{2} + z^{2}} \vert < \epsilon.\] > At this purpose, we just note that \(|x|=\sqrt{x^{2}}\leq\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}}\) (and similarly for \(|y|\) and \(|z|\)), so that > \[\vert \frac{xyz}{x^{2} + y^{2} + z^{2}} \vert\leq \sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}}, \] > and thus \(\delta_{\epsilon}<\epsilon\) does the job. >5) In this case, we have the quotient of polynomials (continuous functions) whose denominator does not vanish when \((x,y)=(0,0)\), so that the limit can be computed putting \((x,y)=(0,0)\).

  2. Prove that the following limits do not exist: \[ \begin{split} 1) &\qquad \lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow(0,0)} \frac{x^{2}+\sin^{2}(y)}{2x^{2}+y^{2}} \\ & \\ 2) & \qquad \lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow(0,0)} \frac{x^{2}y\mathrm{e}^{y}}{x^{4}+4y^{2}} \\ & \\ 3) & \qquad \lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow(0,0)} \frac{x\cos(xy)}{3x^{3}+y} \\ & \\ 4) & \lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow(0,0)} \frac{\sin(2x) - 2x +y}{x^{3}+y} \qquad \\ & \\ 5) & \lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow(0,0)} \frac{\cos(x) - 1 + \frac{x^{2}}{2}}{x^{4}+y} . \qquad .\end{split} \] >[!note]- Solution >1) Evaluate what happens when \(y=mx\) and \(x\rightarrow 0\). >2) Evaluate what happens when \(y=mx^{2}\) and \(x\rightarrow 0\). >3) Evaluate what happens when \(y=mx\) and \(x\rightarrow 0\). >4) Evaluate what happens when \(y=-x^{3}+2x\) and \(x\rightarrow 0\), and then what happens when \(x=0\) and \(y\rightarrow 0\). >5) Evaluate what happens when \(y=-x^{4}+y^{2}\) and \(x\rightarrow 0\), and then what happens when \(x=0\) and \(y\rightarrow 0\).

  3. Determine the points at which \((\mathbb{R}^{2},\mathbb{R}^{2},f,\mathbb{R})\) is continuous when \[ \begin{split} 1)\quad & f(x,y) = \left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{x^{2}y^{3}}{2x^{2}+y^{2}} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \\ 1 & (x,y)=(0,0)\end{matrix}\right. \\ & \\ 2)\quad & f(x,y) = \left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{xy}{x^{2}+xy + y^{2}} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \\ 0 & (x,y)=(0,0) .\end{matrix}\right. \\ & \\ 3) \quad & f(x,y)= \left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{xy}{\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \\ 0 & (x,y)=(0,0) .\end{matrix}\right. \\ & \\ 4) \quad & f(x,y)= \left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{x^{4} + x^{2}y - y^{3}}{\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \\ 0 & (x,y)=(0,0) .\end{matrix}\right. \\ & \\ 5) \quad & f(x,y)= \left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{\sin(x-y)}{\mathrm{e}^{x} - \mathrm{e}^{y}} & (x,y)\neq(c,c) \\ 1 & (x,y)=(c,c) .\end{matrix}\right. \end{split}\] >[!note]- Solution >1) Outside \((0,0)\), the function is continuous because it is the quotient of polynomials whose denominator never vanishes. Evaluating what happens when \(y=mx\), and \(x\rightarrow 0\), we obtain \(0\). Therefore, even if the limit for \((x,y)\rightarrow(0,0)\) existed, it would be equal to \(0\neq f(0,0)=1\), so that the function is not continuous at \((0,0)\). >2) Outside \((0,0)\), the function is continuous for essentially the same reasons of point 1). Evaluating what happens when \(y=mx\), and \(x\rightarrow 0\), we obtain \(\frac{m}{1+m+m^{2}}\), which means the function is not continuous at \((0,0)\). >3) Outside \((0,0)\), the function is continuous for essentially the same reasons of point 1). Since > \[\left|\frac{xy}{\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}} \right|\leq \frac{x^{2} + y^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}}=\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}},\] > it follows that \(f\) is continuous also at \((0,0)\). >4) Following the steps of point 3), we can prove that \(f\) is continuous everywhere. >5) Outside the diagonal, the function is continuous for essentially the same reason of point 1). To investigate what happens on the diagonal, it is best to write > \[\frac{\sin(x-y)}{\mathrm{e}^{x}-\mathrm{e}^{y}}=\mathrm{e}^{-y}\,\frac{\sin(x-y)}{\mathrm{e}^{x-y}-1}.\] > Then, \(f(x,y)\) can be written as the product \(g(x,y)\,h(x,y)\), with \(g(x,y)=y\) a continuous function, and \(h(x,y)=K\circ F(x,y)\) with \(F(x,y)=x-y\) a continuous function, and \(K(t)=\frac{\sin(t)}{\mathrm{e}^{t}-1}\) a function such that > \[\lim_{t\rightarrow 0}K(t)=\lim_{t\rightarrow 0} \frac{\sin(t)}{\mathrm{e}^{t}-1}=1,\] > where we have used de Lâ€™HÃ´pital rule to compute the limit. Therefore, we can apply the properties of limit to conclude that > \[\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow(c,c)}g(x,y)h(x,y)=\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow(c,c)}g(x,y)\left(K\circ F(x,y)\right)= \mathrm{e}^{-c}\lim_{t\rightarrow 0}K(t)=\mathrm{e}^{-c},\] > so that, on the diagonal, \(f\) is continuous only at \((0,0)\).

In \((0,0)\), we need to apply [[2.3 Derivatives and Taylorâ€™s theorem for multivariable functions#^c8b7dc|the definition]] of partial derivative as a limit. Since the numerator is \(xy\), both partial derivatives are \(0\). >3) Outside (0,0), we take partial derivative as in the one-dimensional case. In \((0,0)\), we need to apply [[2.3 Derivatives and Taylorâ€™s theorem for multivariable functions#^c8b7dc|the definition]] of partial derivative as a limit.   2. Compute the partial derivatives and study the differentiability of \((\mathbb{R}^{2},\mathbb{R}^{2},f,\mathbb{R})\) when \[ \begin{split} 1)\quad & f(x,y) = \left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{x^{2}y^{2}}{x^{4}+y^{4}} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \\ 0 & (x,y)=(0,0)\end{matrix}\right. \\ & \\ 2)\quad & f(x,y) = \left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{x^{2}y^{4}}{x^{2} + y^{2}} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \\ 0 & (x,y)=(0,0) .\end{matrix}\right. \\ & \\ 3) \quad & f(x,y)= \left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{x^{2}+y}{\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \\ 0 & (x,y)=(0,0) .\end{matrix}\right. \\ & \\ 4) \quad & f(x,y)= \left\{\begin{matrix}\frac{2xy}{x^{2} + y^{2}} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \\ 0 & (x,y)=(0,0) .\end{matrix}\right. \end{split}\] >[!note]- Solution >1) Outside \((0,0)\), the function is differentiable because it is the quotient of differentiable functions whose denominator never vanishes. At \((0,0)\), using [[2.3 Derivatives and Taylorâ€™s theorem for multivariable functions#^c8b7dc|the definition]] of partial derivative as a limit, we obtain that both partial derivatives are \(0\). Consequently, the differentiability of \(f\) at \((0,0)\) [[2.3 Derivatives and Taylorâ€™s theorem for multivariable functions#^801be3|depends on the validity]] of the following limit > \[\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow(0,0)}\frac{x^{2}y^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}(x^{4}+y^{4})}=0.\] > However, instead of evaluating the previous limit, we may check what happens when we reach \((0,0)\) along the \(x\) axis or along the parabola \(y=x^{2}\) to check that \(f\) is not continuous at \((0,0)\). Consequently, \(f\) can not be differentiable at the origin. >2) Outside \((0,0)\), the function is differentiable for the same reason as in point 1). At \((0,0)\), both partial derivatives vanish when computed using the definition through limits. Recaling that \(x^{2}\leq x^{2} + y^{2}\), that \(y^{2}\leq x^{2}+y^{2}\), and that \(y^{4}=y^{2}y^{2}\), we obtain > \[\frac{x^{2}y^{4}}{x^{2} + y^{2}}\leq (x^{2} + y^{2})^{3},\] > which means that \(f\) is differentiable at \((0,0)\). >3) Outside \((0,0)\), the function is differentiable for the same reason as in point 1). At \((0,0)\), using the definition of partial derivative as a limit, we conclude that the partial derivative along \(x\) is \(1\), but the partial derivative along \(y\) does not exist: > \[\begin{split} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(0,0)&:=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0}\frac{h^{2}}{|h|}=1 \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(0,0)&:=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0}\frac{h}{|h|}=\nexists .\end{split}\] > Since one of the partial derivatives does not exist at \((0,0)\), the function can not be differentiable at \((0,0)\). An alternative proof of the non-differentiability of \(f\) at \((0,0)\) can be obtained evaluating what happens when we reach \((0,0)\) along \(y=\lambda x\). >4) Outside \((0,0)\), the function is differentiable for the same reason as in point 1). At \((0,0)\), using the definition of partial derivative as a limit, we obtain that both vanish. However, evaluating what happens when we reach \((0,0)\) along \(y=\lambda x\), we conclude that \(f\) is not continuous at \((0,0)\), and thus it is not differentiable there.

  5. What can we conclude? >[!note]- Solution >Outside \((0,0)\), the partial derivatives can be computed following the rules of the one-dimensional case.
